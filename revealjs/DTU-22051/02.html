The Nyquist frequency
The frequency limit of a discrete signal with a given sampling rate $(f_s)$ is called the Nyquist frequency $(f_N)$: \[ f_s = 2 \cdot f_N \] or \[ f_s/2 = f_N \]
Implications for sampling of continuous signals
A frequency of $f_{sig}$ to be sampled with a sampling frequency of $f_s$ must be smaller than the Nyquist frequency: \[ f_{sig} < f_N \leftrightarrow f_s > 2 \cdot f_{signal} \]

Aliasing

...frequencies that are (not) there...

Frequencies that are (not) there: Aliasing

  • At a given sampling frequency $F_s$, the Nyquist frequency $F_N$ is $F_s/2$
  • Any component $[-F_s/2, F_s/2]$ gets 'folded back' into the interval $[-F_s/2, F_s/2]$

⇨ The frequency outside the interval gets identified as a lower frequency - it gets an 'alias'

Quickie Nr 1

Take a few minutes to answer the following questions:
  • What determines the minimum sampling frequency you have to choose to sample an analogue signal?

    • The maximum frequency contained in the signal

  • Why is a digital spectrum periodic?

    • It is a consequence of multiplication with a delta pulse train in the time domain, leading to a convolution of the spectrum with a delta pulse train in the frequency domain.

  • How do you reconstruct a digital signal into an analogue one?

    • Bandpass filtering in the frequency domain (same as convolution of all samples with a sinc function in the time domain)

  • How many samples does a time signal of duration T = 1 s have when sampled at fs = 44100 Hz?

    • N = T Â· fs = 44100

  • What do you do to avoid aliasing when digitizing an analogue signal?

    • Low-pass filter the analogue signal (anti-alias filtering)
